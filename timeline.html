What Is Gematria

Gematria is the practice of assigning numerical values to letters and words. The word comes from the Greek geometria, but the practice predates Greece. It ran through Hebrew mysticism, Islamic scholarship, and the esoteric traditions of every civilization that left written records.

The premise is simple: every letter is represented by a number. Every word has a sum. Words that share the same sum share a hidden relationship, a resonance beneath the surface of language.

Have you ever wondered why there are 26 letters in the English alphabet? In Hebrew, every letter is also a number. Both alphabets trace back to the same ancient Semitic script, Phoenician. That root runs through Babylon, the birthplace of civilization, where number and word were never separate things. In the ancient count, Babylon itself reduces to 26. The sacred mathematics was already in the soil. In the Hebrew tradition that grew from it, the letters never stopped being numbers. The Sefer Yetzirah, the Book of Formation, one of the oldest texts of Kabbalah, teaches that God created the world by combining three instruments: the letter, the number, and the word. Creation itself was an act of language. The number 26 holds the four letter name of that creator, the Tetragrammaton: YHWH. Yod (10) + Heh (5) + Vav (6) + Heh (5) = 26.

Ancient mystics called YHWH the name of the creator. Scientists, working from the opposite direction entirely, discovered that this exact number, and this exact four unit structure, is encoded in the molecule that carries the blueprint of every living thing on Earth. The name of God and the geometry of DNA share the same architecture. This is THE arithmetic.

The Cipher Systems

Gematria operates through multiple cipher systems, different methods of assigning numerical values to letters. Each cipher reveals a different layer of encoding. When the same number appears across multiple ciphers, multiple names, and multiple events, coincidence stops being a workable explanation. What remains is pattern. The four foundational ciphers are sometimes called the four pillars. Beyond them lie ancient systems with their own lineage and logic. This story draws from all of them. Ordinal: The First Pillar
The simplest and most direct cipher. A=1, B=2, C=3, through Z=26. It maps each letter to its position in the English alphabet. Ordinal is the baseline, the first lens you apply. It often reveals the most obvious connections: God = 26, Sean = 39, October = 78. When ordinal matches appear between two words, names, or dates, it is the most fundamental kind of resonance, a direct, unmanipulated alignment.
Reverse Ordinal: The Mirror Pillar
The alphabet reversed: A=26, B=25, C=24, through Z=1. Reverse ordinal reads the alphabet from the other direction, as if viewing it in a mirror. It reveals what is hidden when you look at something from the opposite side. In Hebrew, the language that formalized gematria, text is read right to left. The reverse cipher honors this principle. It often surfaces connections that ordinal cannot see, the shadow side of a word, the meaning that faces the other way.
Reduction: The Essence Pillar
Each letter's ordinal value is reduced to a single digit by summing its digits: A=1, B=2 ... J=10→1, K=11→2, S=19→10→1, and so on. Reduction strips a word down to its numerological essence, the vibrational root beneath the surface number. It is the cipher most closely aligned with traditional numerology and often reveals the deepest symbolic connections. Carbon = 26. Lead Into Gold = 55. Mother = 34.

Gematria Through the Ages

Gematria is a thread that runs through every major civilization on Earth, independently developed, independently documented, used as a key to the hidden architecture of language, creation, and power. The details differ across cultures and centuries. The principle holds: letters are numbers, and numbers reveal a structure that ordinary reading cannot see.

Babylon, ~800 BCE. The oldest confirmed use of letter-number encoding dates to ancient Assyria. King Sargon II commissioned his palace at Dur-Sharrukin with a perimeter wall measuring exactly 16,283 cubits, the precise numerical value of his name in the Babylonian letter-number system. He did not do this for decoration. He did it because he believed that embedding a name's number in stone bound the structure to the power of that name. While Assyrian-Babylonian scribes used cuneiform, a completely separate logographic system, the Phoenician alphabet was developing in parallel among the Semitic peoples of the Levant: the direct ancestor of Hebrew, Greek, Latin, and every Western script including the one you are reading now.

Hebrew Kabbalah, 3rd to 13th century CE. The practice was formalized as a systematic spiritual method within Jewish mysticism. The Sefer Yetzirah (Book of Formation) describes creation as God combining the letter, the number, and the word as the three instruments of existence, 22 Hebrew letters and 10 primal numbers (the sefirot) as the raw materials of creation itself. The Zohar, the central text of Kabbalah compiled in 13th-century Spain, uses gematria extensively to link Torah passages: if two verses share the same numerical value, they are spiritually connected regardless of their surface meaning. The Kabbalists built entire cosmological maps from these values, the Tree of Life, the sefirot, the structure of heaven and earth, all rooted in the arithmetic of letters. They understood gematria not as interpretation but as engineering: reading the blueprint creation was built from.

Greek isopsephy. The Greeks developed the same practice independently and called it isopsephy, from isos (equal) and psephos (pebble, referring to counting stones). Every letter of the Greek alphabet held a numerical value, and philosophers, poets, and oracle priests used these values to find hidden meanings in sacred texts and names. Pythagoras taught that number was the foundation of all reality, not metaphor but structural fact. The proof is in Revelation: the number value of NERON KAISAR, Nero Caesar written in Greek letters, equals 666. Most modern scholars now believe Revelation was written in deliberate code, and the "number of the beast" was always a reference to the Roman emperor, hidden in plain numerical sight. The ancient world was fluent in this language. We forgot how to read it.

Islamic abjad. Arabic has its own letter-number correspondence system called abjad, derived from the same Phoenician root as Hebrew gematria. The 28 letters of the Arabic alphabet each carry a numerical value, and Islamic scholars have used abjad in Quranic interpretation, sacred naming, analysis of divine attributes, and architecture. The 99 names of God were studied through their abjad values. Sufi mystics used numerical equivalence to find hidden connections between divine names and verses. Sacred buildings were proportioned to the numerical values of the words they were meant to embody. The same logic, on a different continent, in a different language, arriving at the same method.

Renaissance hermeticism, 15th to 16th century. When Greek and Arabic manuscripts flooded into Europe, scholars formalized gematria within the Western tradition. Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa's Three Books of Occult Philosophy (1531) catalogued numerical values for Hebrew, Greek, and Latin letters across multiple cipher systems, a framework nearly identical to what modern practitioners use today. John Dee, mathematician and advisor to Queen Elizabeth I, developed a sacred language rooted in the belief that number was the primary channel through which the divine communicated with creation. These were not fringe figures. Agrippa's work was read by every educated person in Europe. Dee corresponded with the most powerful monarchs of his era. The practice sat at the center of Renaissance intellectual life.

Freemasonry. The hermetic tradition passed through the Rosicrucians into Freemasonry, where number symbolism became embedded in ritual, architecture, and initiation structure. The 33 degrees of Scottish Rite Freemasonry. The dimensions of Solomon's Temple as recorded in the Book of Kings, themselves numerically encoded in the original Hebrew. The layout of Washington D.C., designed by Freemason surveyors, encodes geometric relationships between the Capitol, the White House, and the Washington Monument that Masonic architects understood to be numerologically deliberate. The intent to encode number into physical space was not secret. It was philosophy made architecture.

The thread runs unbroken from Sargon's palace wall in 800 BCE to the community of researchers working in real time today. Different languages. Different centuries. Different continents. The same practice, the same conviction: that letters are numbers, that numbers are not arbitrary, and that somewhere beneath the surface of language a structure is waiting, patient, precise, and unchanged, for anyone willing to learn how to read it.

What Is Numerology

Numerology is the study of the symbolic significance of numbers. Where gematria converts language into number, numerology interprets what those numbers mean. It holds that the universe is fundamentally mathematical. That numbers may be more than a tool humans discovered to describe reality. They may be a structural property of reality itself.

Pythagoras, the Greek mathematician who proved that the squares of a right triangle's legs equal the square of its hypotenuse, also founded a mystery school that taught numbers as living forces. To him, mathematics and mysticism were the same inquiry: what is the universe made of? His answer was number. Twenty-five centuries later, quantum physics arrived at a strikingly similar conclusion.

The core of numerology is the chart. It is calculated from two sources: the full birth name and the birth date. The birth date reduces to a Life Path number, the single most significant number in the chart. It describes the overarching nature and direction of a person's life. The full name at birth encodes three additional numbers: the Expression (all letters), the Soul Urge (vowels only), and the Personality (consonants only). Each represents a different layer of who a person is. The public self. The private drive. The outer impression. Together they form a portrait that is both specific and structural.

Where the Sciences Converge

Multiple scientific disciplines, developed with no awareness of each other, are arriving at the same conclusions the ancient numerological traditions intuited thousands of years ago:

The Theoretical: What Remains Open

Simulation theory. The idea that reality may be a computed information system, advanced by philosopher Nick Bostrom and discussed by physicists including Neil deGrasse Tyson, suggests that if the universe is computational, it would necessarily run on mathematical rules. Recurring numerical patterns in names, dates, and events would then be expected features of a system built on code, not anomalies to be explained away.

Morphic resonance. Biologist Rupert Sheldrake's hypothesis proposes that nature has a kind of memory, that patterns, once established, become easier to repeat through "morphic fields" that operate outside conventional space-time. If true, recurring numerical signatures in a person's life may be the expression of a morphic field, a pattern that, once activated, tends to replicate itself across events, relationships, and timelines.

Carl Jung's synchronicity. Jung defined synchronicity as "meaningful coincidence": events connected not by cause and effect but by meaning. He proposed that the psyche and the physical world are two expressions of a single underlying reality he called the unus mundus. In Jung's view, the inner event and the outer event are connected through a layer of reality that runs beneath linear causation.

The holographic principle. Theoretical physicist David Bohm proposed that the universe operates like a hologram, where every part contains information about the whole. In a holographic universe, a name encodes a person. The letters are a compressed expression of everything that person is, was, and will become.

What Is a Sacred Masculine Initiator

In many traditions, the sacred masculine initiator is the older man who teaches a younger man how to be a man, not through domination or control, but through presence, integrity, and example. He is the one who shows you that strength is not the absence of vulnerability but the willingness to face it. He holds the space that a father could not or did not hold. He does not replace the father. He completes what the father left unfinished.

The sacred masculine initiator is not always conscious of the role he plays. He does not need to know he is initiating. What matters is that he lives in a way that gives the younger man permission to become something he could not have imagined alone. He models what sovereignty looks like in a body, how a man carries responsibility, how a man treats the people around him, how a man stays when it would be easier to leave. The initiation is not a ceremony. It is the accumulated weight of a thousand ordinary moments in which one man shows another what it looks like to be whole.

The Role He Played

Mike was my boss. That was the surface-level title. What he actually was, what he became without either of us naming it, was the masculine initiator I never had. My father was absent in the way that mattered most: not physically gone, but emotionally unreachable. Mike filled the shape of that absence without trying. He showed me what it looked like to lead without needing to be feared. To show up every day with the same quiet consistency. To hold people accountable because he believed they were capable of more, not because he needed to be in charge.

He was the kind of man who made everyone around him better by being in the room. Not loud about it. Not performative. Just there, in the way that mattered. The staff trips to Whistler, the daily rhythms of work, the small moments where he would say exactly the right thing at exactly the right time, these were the initiations. I did not know I was being taught. I only knew I wanted to be more like him.

There is a photograph from a staff trip to Whistler. A whiskey jack, a grey jay, the bird that lands on your hand if you hold still enough, is flying directly out of Mike's outstretched palm. His arm is extended, relaxed, steady. The bird trusted him enough to land, and the photo caught the exact moment it lifted off. I got his initials tattooed on my hand: MN = 27. On the other hand: WWND = 34, What Would Nyuis Do. I did not know gematria then. I did not know that 27 and 34 would become two of the most important numbers in this story. I only know now what those numbers meant.

The Sudden Departure

Mike died suddenly. There was no warning, no long illness, no chance to say the things you always assume you will have time to say. One day he was there, the steady center, the man whose presence made the room make sense, and the next day he was gone. The shape of that loss was identical to the shape of my mother's death: safe love, vanishing without warning. The nervous system recorded them both the same way. Two disappearances. Two people who made the world feel survivable, removed from it without explanation.

The whiskey jack lands on the hand that holds still. Mike held still his entire life. And when he left, the bird kept flying, into the hands of everyone he had quietly taught to hold still too.
